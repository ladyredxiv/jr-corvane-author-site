Why Every Kingdom Edits Its Own History

The most durable lies contain enough truth to survive scrutiny.

By J.R. Corvane · July 19, 2026 · 7 minute read

History likes a clean ending.

A war is won. A monster is slain. A crown is restored. The dead are counted, the heroes are named, and the story settles into something solid enough to carve into stone.

Reality is rarely so cooperative.

When I began building the world of The Sundering Bloodlines, I kept returning to one question: What happens when the story holding a kingdom together is not entirely true?

Not a clumsy lie. Not a conspiracy every sensible person should have noticed generations ago. Something more difficult—and, I think, more believable. A story created from fear, necessity, incomplete knowledge, and choices that may once have seemed defensible.

The most durable lies contain enough truth to survive scrutiny.

The Story a Kingdom Needs

Perhaps there really was a war. Perhaps people really did sacrifice themselves. Perhaps the kingdom standing afterward genuinely needed a shared account of what had happened if it was going to rebuild.

But history is not only a record of events. It is also a tool. It tells people who saved them, who betrayed them, who deserves to rule, and whose suffering can be treated as the acceptable price of survival.

That makes history a form of power.

The official history of a nation rarely preserves every contradiction. It cannot easily admit that its founders were frightened, that its victory was incomplete, or that the people it condemned may have understood the danger better than those who survived to write the record.

Kingdoms prefer martyrs who do not argue with the monument.

They prefer villains who remain conveniently dead.

Most of all, they prefer endings.

An unfinished catastrophe is politically difficult. A defeated enemy can be commemorated. A wounded enemy that may someday return demands vigilance, sacrifice, and uncomfortable questions about whether the people in power truly know what they are doing.

So the wound becomes a victory. The warning becomes a prophecy. The compromise becomes destiny.

Given enough time, the version created to hold frightened people together becomes sacred. Children learn it before they are old enough to question it. Institutions build their authority upon it. Families inherit honor or disgrace according to the roles assigned to their ancestors.

Eventually, exposing the lie threatens more than the people who first told it. It threatens everyone whose identity depends upon believing it.

That is where history becomes especially dangerous.

Who Gets Remembered

I am fascinated by the distance between the people who endure an event and the version of them preserved afterward.

History remembers symbols more easily than human beings.

A person becomes “the chosen one,” “the traitor,” “the last king,” or “the twelve who saved the world.” The title survives because it is easier to carry than the full, inconvenient truth of a life.

But what happens to the people who do not fit the shape of the legend?

What happens when twelve people enter a valley and history decides only four of them mattered?

That question became the heart of The Uncounted, the free story set generations before Blood of the Bound. It also reaches into the larger series. The world of The Sundering Bloodlines is crowded with consequences inherited from people whose names were erased, rewritten, or turned into warnings by those who came after them.

Some were forgotten accidentally.

Others were forgotten because remembering them correctly would have made the official story impossible to defend.

A Lie Can Still Save Someone

The part that interests me most is that a false history does not require a purely evil person at its beginning.

Sometimes a lie is told because the truth would break people who have already survived too much. Sometimes the person telling it believes they are purchasing a generation of peace. Sometimes they know the debt will eventually come due and can only hope the future will be stronger when it does.

That does not make the lie harmless.

It makes it tragic.

A comforting story can help one generation rebuild while ensuring the next generation is unprepared for what was buried beneath it. The protection becomes a prison. The necessary compromise becomes an institution. People born centuries later inherit both the safety the lie created and the cost of finally confronting it.

There are no clean hands in that kind of history.

That moral complication is one of the reasons I love epic fantasy. The genre gives us ancient wars, broken crowns, forbidden archives, and magic old enough to have become religion. But beneath that scale are deeply human decisions: what we conceal, what we preserve, and what we convince ourselves was necessary.

When the Truth Comes Due

Blood of the Bound begins at the moment a comfortable history can no longer contain what actually happened.

Its characters inherit stories about who they are, what their blood means, and which institutions deserve their loyalty. Some desperately want those stories to be true. Others have built entire lives around rejecting them.

None of them has the complete account.

The truth, when it emerges, does not simply correct a history lesson. It changes who holds power. It alters old loyalties. It forces people to reconsider which debts belong to them—and whether being born into an inheritance means they are obligated to fulfill it.

That is the danger of digging beneath a kingdom’s founding myth.

You may discover that the monster was never truly defeated.

You may discover that the traitors were trying to save you.

Or you may discover that the lie was never intended to last this long.

Every kingdom edits its own history.
The real question is what happens when history begins editing back.
