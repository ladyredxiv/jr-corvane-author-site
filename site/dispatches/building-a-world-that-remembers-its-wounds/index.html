Behind the Veil

Building a World That Remembers Its Wounds

A catastrophe should leave more behind than a ruined tower and a date in a chronicle.

By J.R. Corvane · June 21, 2026 · 6 minute read

I like my fantasy worlds old.

Not merely decorated with ruins, but shaped by what those ruins mean. I want roads that bend around places no one will enter, children’s rhymes whose warnings have outlived their explanations, and borders drawn by wars the living remember incorrectly.

A convincing past should exert pressure on the present.

When I began building the world of The Sundering Bloodlines, I did not want its history to sit politely in a timeline. The Sundering needed to remain alive in the habits, fears, institutions, and silences of people born generations later.

The catastrophe ended. Its consequences did not.

Scars Are More Than Ruins

Ruins are wonderful. Give me a fallen citadel or a sealed door covered in an alphabet no living scholar can read and I will happily follow the story inside.

But physical remnants are only one way a world remembers.

A disaster changes where people settle and what they eat. It alters trade routes, inheritance laws, burial customs, and the stories adults use to frighten children into obedience. A war can decide which names sound noble centuries later. A magical catastrophe can turn an ordinary color, gesture, or birthmark into an omen.

The most interesting scars are often invisible to the people carrying them.

They do not think, “We do this because our ancestors were afraid.” They call it tradition. They call it common sense. They may not know that another way of living was ever possible.

That is how history becomes texture rather than exposition. The past appears in what a character assumes, what they refuse to touch, and which questions they have been taught not to ask.

Memory Is Not the Same as Truth

A world can remember an event vividly and still remember it wrong.

Communities preserve the past for reasons. They need heroes, warnings, explanations, and a shared account of why the world looks as it does. Over time, complicated events harden into stories that can be repeated at festivals or carved beneath statues.

The details that survive are not necessarily the most accurate. They are the most useful.

One kingdom remembers a battle as liberation. Across the border, it is remembered as an invasion. One family inherits honor from an ancestor’s sacrifice; another inherits exile from the same afternoon. Neither version needs to be wholly false for the contradiction to endure.

This is fertile ground for fantasy because magic introduces another kind of uncertainty. What happens when myth preserves a truth scholars dismissed? What happens when the official record is accurate about what occurred but catastrophically wrong about why?

I love the moment a character discovers that the old story was not foolish. It was incomplete.

Let the Past Be Inconvenient

History becomes dramatic when it interferes.

If an ancient war matters only when a scholar delivers a lecture, it is background. If that war determines whom a character is permitted to marry, whether a village will shelter them, or why the safest road has been abandoned, then it is part of the plot.

The same is true of old magic. Power becomes more interesting when it carries inherited assumptions and obligations. Who was allowed to wield it? Who paid for it? What did people build around the belief that it had vanished?

In Blood of the Bound, the past is not waiting to be discovered like a pristine artifact. It has been handled. Pieces are missing. Institutions have been built over the gaps, and people have arranged their identities around what they believe happened.

Revealing the truth cannot simply satisfy curiosity. It must inconvenience the present.

It must make someone less certain of their loyalty. It must threaten an authority that seemed permanent. It must ask whether the descendants of those who benefited from an old injustice owe anything to the descendants of those who paid for it.

Otherwise, history is only scenery.

A World Is What People Carry Forward

Worldbuilding often begins with maps, calendars, pantheons, and systems of magic. I love all of those things. I can disappear into a research tunnel with very little encouragement.

But a world becomes real to me through inheritance.

People inherit recipes and prejudices. They inherit sacred objects whose original purposes have been forgotten. They inherit lullabies, debts, titles, forbidden names, and fears that feel instinctive because no one remembers learning them.

They also inherit resilience. A wounded culture is not only its suffering. People adapt. They make beauty from what remains. They turn survival into celebration, change rituals that no longer serve them, and find humor in places their ancestors knew only as dangerous.

That matters. A world shaped by catastrophe should still contain ordinary tenderness. Someone repairs a roof. Someone saves a seat at the table. Someone teaches a child the old song, perhaps changing one line without realizing it.

History lives there, too.

A world remembers its wounds through more than monuments.
It remembers in the lives people build around the places that never healed.
